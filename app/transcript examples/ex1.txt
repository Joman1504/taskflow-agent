Professor: Now, let’s move into today’s topic: the Constitutional Convention of 1787.
Professor: After the weaknesses of the Articles of Confederation became apparent—particularly the lack of a strong central government—delegates from the states convened in Philadelphia to revise the system. However, what began as a revision quickly turned into the drafting of an entirely new Constitution.
Professor: One of the major debates at the convention was representation. Larger states favored the Virginia Plan, which proposed representation based on population, while smaller states supported the New Jersey Plan, which called for equal representation.
Professor: The compromise that emerged, known as the Great Compromise, established a bicameral legislature with proportional representation in the House of Representatives and equal representation in the Senate.
Professor: Another key issue was how to count enslaved individuals for purposes of representation and taxation. This led to the Three-Fifths Compromise, which counted enslaved people as three-fifths of a person. It’s important to understand both the political motivations behind this compromise and its moral implications.
Professor: The delegates also debated the structure of the executive branch. Ultimately, they agreed on a single president, with checks and balances built into the system to prevent any one branch from becoming too powerful.
Professor: The Constitution that resulted from the convention established a federal system, dividing power between the national government and the states, and included mechanisms such as checks and balances and separation of powers.
Professor: However, the Constitution did not immediately go into effect. It required ratification by the states, which sparked intense debate between Federalists, who supported the Constitution, and Anti-Federalists, who feared a strong central government.
Professor: The Federalist Papers, written by Alexander Hamilton, James Madison, and John Jay, were instrumental in advocating for ratification. They remain a key source for understanding the intentions behind the Constitution.
Student: Professor, were there any states that strongly opposed ratification?
Professor: Yes, several states were initially resistant, particularly those concerned about the absence of a bill of rights. This concern ultimately led to the addition of the first ten amendments shortly after ratification.
